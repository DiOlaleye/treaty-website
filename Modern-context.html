Modern Context

More History

Treaty 5 was signed on September 24, 1875, at Norway House, Manitoba. It was signed by representatives of the Canadian government and various First Nations groups, including the Cree and Ojibwe peoples. The treaty was intended to establish peaceful relations between the Indigenous peoples and the Canadian government, as well as to facilitate the settlement of European settlers in the region. The treaty outlined the rights and responsibilities of both parties, including provisions for land use, hunting and fishing rights, and the establishment of reserves for Indigenous communities. It also included promises of education, healthcare, and other forms of support for the Indigenous peoples. However, the implementation of Treaty 5 has been a source of ongoing controversy and debate, with many Indigenous communities arguing that the terms of the treaty were not fully honored by the Canadian government.

How it's being implemented today

Today, Treaty 5 continues to be a significant and contentious issue in Canada. Many Indigenous communities that are signatories to Treaty 5 have expressed concerns about the ongoing implementation of the treaty and the fulfillment of its promises by the Canadian government. Issues such as land rights, resource extraction, and the provision of services to Indigenous communities remain at the forefront of discussions around Treaty 5. Indigenous leaders and activists continue to advocate for greater recognition of their rights and for the fulfillment of the promises made in the treaty, while also seeking to address historical injustices and promote reconciliation between Indigenous peoples and the Canadian government.
